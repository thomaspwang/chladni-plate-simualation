Overview

Give a high-level overview of what you implemented in this project. Think about what you've built as a whole. Share your thoughts on what interesting things you've learned from completing the project.


In this project, we implemented cloth physics using a spring mesh model, and we added various textures and collision behaviors. First, we implemented the mesh itself simply by iterating over a grid of points. We then implemented physics modeling via Verlet integration, where we approximate the behavior of each point mass in the spring mesh under spring forces, gravity, and pin constraints. We then implemented more realistic behavior by implementing logical collision behavior with spheres and planes, as well as self collisions. We optimized our self-collision implementation by using a hash map to only have to check collisions between point-masses nearby to one another. Finally, we explored GLSL by implementing and comparing diffuse shading, Blinn-Phong shading, texture mapping, bump mapping, displacement mapping, and mirror shading.

Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

With a very low ks = 50 N/m, we observe that the cloth is extra stretchy, so it is able to bend out of the normal catenary shape in the locations off the pinned diagonals. This appears as "ridges" along the pinned diagonals. With a very high ks = 100000 N/m, we observe that the cloth is extra taut, hanging relatively high and with minimal deflection.

What about for density?

Changing density has the inverse effect to changing the spring constant. This is because a higher density increases the relative strength of gravity (vs. spring forces), as does lowering the spring constant to weaken spring forces. With a very low density = 1 g/cm^2, we observe again the taut cloth; with a very high density = 100 g/cm^2 we observe the same "ridges" we see with a low ks.

Part 5: Cloth Sim
Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

A shader program is a parallelizable nugget that computes colors and displacements across a scene. Here, we consider vertex shaders and fragment shaders. Vertex shaders compute and apply vertex displacements, and use this to compute the fragment position, texture, and normal information required for fragment shaders. The fragment shaders then use the outputs of the vertex shaders to compute the screen color of each fragment based on position, normal, and external lighting.

Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

The Blinn-Phong shading model approximates lighting as three primary components. The first term is ambient lighting, which captures general softening of shadows and multiple reflections of light. The second term is diffuse lighting, which computes color based on the matte color of objects in the scene. The final term is specular lighting, which captures the bright, concentrated reflections of light off of shiny objects such as latex or metal.
